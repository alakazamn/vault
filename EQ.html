EQ

An equalizer, or EQ, affects the balance of frequencies within a sound. Sounds are based on a fundamental pitch and a set of harmonics which make up the timbre, or tone of the sound. By affecting the balance of these harmonics, the tone of the sound can be changed.

Equalizer Types

Parametric

A parametric EQ provides continuous control over frequency and boost/cut. It may have a continuous control for Q, or the Q may be a predefined set of options.

Graphic

A graphic EQ gives you boost/cut control over a predetermined set of bands. The bandwidth / Q of the bands are also not adjustable.

Filters

Low-Pass

A low-pass filter is an EQ adjustment that cuts high frequencies, allowing low frequencies to pass through.

High-Pass

A high-pass filter is an EQ adjustment that cuts low frequencies, allowing high frequencies to pass through.

  • High-pass filters can be used for cleaning purposes (e.g., clearing rumble).

Bandpass 

A bandpass filter is a combination of a low pass and a high pass filter.

Shelving 

A shelving filter is a boost or cut at either the high end (high shelf) or low end (low shelf) of the frequency spectrum. The boost or cut tapers off to a preset level at which it continues to the end of the frequency spectrum.[1]

  • A low shelf is used for shaping sound or controlling the proximity effect.
    • On acoustic guitar, a high pass filter might be used in tandem with a low shelf to clear rumble and the proximity effect, respectively.[2]
  • A high shelf from 1.5k and above is employed by Chris Lorde-Alge and John Hanes / Serban Ghenea to "get a brighter overall tone"[3]
    • "Not edgy since he is boosting around 1k using a shelf and not, say, much harsher 4k."

Notch 

A notch filter has a very narrow bandwidth, and is thus used to surgically boost or cut specific frequencies.

  • Can be used to remove 60- or 50- hz hum
  • Often used in film or broadcast sound where narrow-band problems are likely to occur[1-1]
