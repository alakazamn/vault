Sound

In physics, a sound is a periodic variation in atmospheric pressure, also known as a sound-pressure wave[1] or simply sound wave, which is able to be heard. It is caused by the change in air pressure created by a vibrating object.

Compression and Rarefraction

A sound-pressure wave is created by a vibrating object: as an object vibrates back and forth, it compresses and air. As it moves forward, it pushes air forward, creating an area of high pressure in a process known as compression or condensation. As it moves backwards, the additional space allows air molecules to spread out, creating an area of low pressure in a process called rarefaction.[2-1] This process repeats many times in one second, creating patterns of these high and low pressure variations.

This creates a sort of chain reaction, as the areas of high pressure continue to push against areas of low pressure in the same direction, projecting the wave outwards in a process known as wave propagation.[4]


A metaphor for this process is the ripples created from dropping a pebble in a pond.[2-2]

Note from the above diagram that it is the pressure changes that propagate outwards, not the air molecules themselves. Another metaphor is rows of people in a crowd[5] One row pushing on the next creates a chain reaction of pushing that ripples up to the front, but nobody ends up moving that far.

 

Waveforms

A waveform is "the graphic representation of a sound pressure level or voltage level as it moves through a medium over time"[4-1].

Characteristics of Waveforms

Amplitude

Amplitude is the distance above or below the baseline of a wave.[4-2] Represents the pressure variation or signal level.

Types of Measurements:

  • Peak Amplitude Value-- a measurement of either the maximum positive or negative signal level of a wave
  • Peak-to-Peak Amplitude Value-- the combined measurement of the positive and negative peak signal levels
  • Root-Mean Square (RMS) Value
    • Developed to closer approximate how humans perceive loudness
    • Average of squares of amplitude at points along wave
    • Multiply the peak amplitude value by 12
    • Always positive

Frequency

(see Pitch)

Frequency is how often one cycle of a wave (shown below) repeats in a given period of time. Typically, that period of time is a second. The unit for cycles per second is hz. It is the standard unit for measuring frequency.

Frequency affects perceived pitch. Higher frequencies are perceived as a higher pitches, and a lower frequencies are perceived as lower pitches.[4-3]

Harmonic Content

(see Harmonic Series)
Instruments do not produce single frequencies. Instead, there are many other frequencies, called harmonics or overtones present, though the perceived pitch of a note is the lowest (fundamental) frequency. The balance of harmonics determines the timbre of the sound and allows for differentiation between instruments.[6]

Envelope

(see Envelope)

The envelope of a waveform is the change in amplitude over the duration of the sound. It is usually described by four characteristics:[4-5]

  • Attack (A), the time from the onset of a sound to its peak volume
  • Decay (D), how fast the sound returns to a sustain level after reaching its peak
  • Sustain (S), how long a sound will last after the decay
  • Release (R), how long the sound will continue after the note is released
