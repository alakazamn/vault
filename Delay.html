Delay

In music production, delay is an effect where a signal is repeated at a later time.[1][2]

Types of Delay

Combing & Flanging (<15ms)

When using delays with extremely short times (microseconds), Phase interactions cause selective equalization at certain frequencies. This is how EQ units work.[2-1]

Combing, or comb-filtering, results from delays less than 15ms which are mixed with the original signal. As the signal moves in and out of phase, constructive and destructive interference occurs, producing uneven peaks and notches.

Flanging results from combing where the delay time is varied over time, typically using a LFO.

Chorusing & Doubling (15 to 35ms)

Chorusing results from combining two identical, but typically time-delayed signals that slightly differ in pitch.

Delays in the 15 to 35ms range are not perceived separately; instead, they produce a thickening or doubling effect as a result of the Haas Effect. See the article on thickening a sound for more examples.

Echo (>35ms)

Delays greater than 35ms are perceived as distinct echoes. Often, these delays will be timed with the tempo of the song.

By feeding the delayed signal back into the original circuit, the signal will continue to repeat. This is an effect known as feedback or regeneration.

Accumulated Delays

The accumulation of echoes to create a sense of interior ambiance is known as reverb.
