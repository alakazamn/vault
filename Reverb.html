Reverb

Reverb is the accumulation of echoes to create a sense of interior ambiance. It is perceived as a persistence of sound after the source has stopped sounding.[1]

In natural reverb, the accumulation of echoes is a result of the sound bouncing off the boundaries of a space. Thus, natural reverb consists of the multiple densely packed, random echoes reflected off of the boundaries of a space. These echoes reach the listener at different times and intensities, giving a subconscious impression of the size, density, and nature of a space. They are broken up into three categories:

Sound heard directly from a source is known as direct sound. As this sound radiates out, it then reflects off of the major boundaries of the room, creating early reflections. Subconsciously, these first few reflections give you an idea of the size and space of a room. As the sound continues to bounce around a space, reverberation consists of the many random reflections that occur between a room's boundaries. They are too dense to hear separately, so the ear perceives them as a single decaying signal.[2]

This effect can also be simulated in analog or digital reverb units.

Controls

Pre-delay

Pre-delay is the time between the direct sound and the first early reflections. By increasing the pre-delay, one can separate the reverb from the direct sound, aiding with clarity.[3]

  • With a long pre-delay, you are increasing the time it takes for the sound to reflect. As such, this will make the sound seem closer to you and farther away from the boundaries of the room.

  • Conversely, using a short pre-delay will make the sound seem farther from you, closer to the boundaries of the room. You are decreasing the time it takes for the sound to reflect.

Common Uses
  • A short pre-delay is often desirable for drums, where the reverb should blend with the tone of the drums to thicken the sound.
  • A short pre-delay helps blend elements, such as backing guitar and keys, into a mix
  • A long pre-delay can be used on vocals and leads, where one hopes to both thicken the timbre and preserve the sound's attack[4]

Diffusion

Diffusion, or density, controls the thickness or coherence of the reverb; in other words, how spread out the echoes are over time.

Diffusion creates a sense of the level of clutter in a room:

  • Low diffusion mirrors an empty room, where individual reflections can remain more intact. This results in less initial build up of density, and a more spread out or "open," and potentially metallic, grainy, or thin sound.

  • In a crowded room (high diffusion), reflections are broken up. As a result, high settings of diffusion mean the reverb will quickly build up in density, for a dense, smooth, lush, or "blurry" sound. [4-1][5][6][7]

Common Uses
  • High settings of diffusion add thickness, and work well on percussion such as snare drums
  • Low to moderate diffusion settings work well on natural sounds, such as vocals or piano, as well as mixes, as they help to preserve the original timbre of a sound.[6-1][7-1]
  • Low diffusion settings take up less space in a mix
  • Low diffusion settings can sound grainy or metallic and may work on electric guitars[4-2]

Decay / Tail

Decay, or the tail is how long it takes for a reverb to finish sounding. It is often coordinated with size, as certain combinations can be perceived as unrealistic.

  • Modern music commonly uses decay times between one and three seconds.
  • A fast decay can help stereoize mono sounds[5-1]

Types of Reverb

Plate

Plate reverbs are giant boxes with thin, metal plates inside. Sound enters the box and vibrates the plate, and the resulting reverberation is recorded by one or more pickups. The result is a dense, sizzly sound. Modern plate reverbs emulate this sound.

Common Uses
  • Vocals
  • Percussion

Spring

Spring reverbs consist of a pickup and a transducer connected by a spring. Some of the sound that reaches the end of the spring is reflected backwards, creating a back-and-forth effect. Thus, the resulting sound has a bouncy character.
