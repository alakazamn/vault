Compression

In music, compression is the process of reducing the dynamic range of a signal, by making quiet parts of the signal louder (upward compresssion), making loud parts of the signal quieter (downward compression), or both. A device that applies compression to a signal is known as a compressor.

Design

Types of Compressors

FET

Field-effect transistors, or FETs, are solid state components that act similarly to vacuum tubes. Though they last longer, they do not have the same sonic character.

FET compressors are built using these components.

List of FET Compressors

Universal Audio 1176

Optical

Optical compressors contain transducers which change sound into light and then back again. This process has unique features which are difficult to replicate with solid state or tube designs: most notably, a "gentle and musical" release curve. As such, optical compressors have a transparent and smooth quality.[1]

List of Optical Compressors
Img1Unit
Teletronix LA-2A

Vari-Mu

Variable-mu, or tube compressors, are compressors which incorporate a vacuum tube in their design. These compressors have the typical benefits of tube equipment: nonlinearity and analog coloration / saturation.[2] The mu stands for "gain or amplification factor."[3]

List of Vari-Mu Compressors
Img1Unit
Manley Stereo Variable MU Limiter Compressor

VCA

Voltage-controlled amplifier, or VCA compressors, rely on transistors to affect the sound. This provides essentially full control over timing functions. As such, these compressors have fast attack and release functions, minimal coloration, high transparency, and are often cheaper than the other types.[4]

Usage

Controls

Threshold

At what level the compressor kicks in.

The knee of a compressor affects the range of the threshold, and thus adjusts the smoothness of the transition:

  • A soft knee, occasionally referred to as over easy, widens the range of the threshold, causing the effect to gradually kick in as level exceeds the threshold and making the effect less noticeable.
    • In other words, there is no single threshold point at which the effect kicks in; rather, it slowly transitions over a range.[5]
  • A hard knee tightens the range of the threshold, causes the effect to kick in quickly above the threshold range and making the effect more noticeable.
    • In other words, there is one singular threshold point at which the effect kicks in; there is no transition.

Note

Attack and knee can seem similar, but they are very different. Knee actually affects the relationship of input level to output level, while attack affects how long the compressor takes to react. In other words, the former affects the target, while the latter defines how long it will take to reach the target.

Ratio

Specifies the relationship between input and output gain over the threshold. For example, a 4:1 compression ratio means that the output gain over the threshold will be a quarter of input gain over the threshold.


[6]

Make-Up Gain

A trim knob. Useful to match average input levels approximately with output levels, as compression decreases the level of a signal, and the louder sounds better effect can deceive you as to whether compression is actually improving your mix.

Timing

(see Envelope)
A compressor typically has attack and release parameters.

  • Attack controls the time it takes for the compressor to react to a signal once it has crossed the threshold.
  • Release controls how long the compressor will maintain its gain reduction before returning back to normal.

The timing of a compressor can be used to enhance the articulation of notes, specifically in vocal production:

A slow attack might help a staccato vocal cut through, a fast attack might be good for smoothing legato notes. Release adjustments are made according to the timing of the vocals, I don't want to pull up too much between the words, so I'd like the release to happen before the word or syllable ends.

- John Hanes

Staccato Vocal

On "This Is Why We Can't Have Nice Things":

It has to do with the makeup gain; the consonant will be squashed but pushed up by makeup gain; the rest of the word will not hit the makeup gain, so you get a staccato kind of compression effect.
The 1176 would be set fast for this, the MH just a bit of smoothing on a slower setting if used at all in this case.

If the performance is already staccato vocals, then the fast settings will help to reinforce that.

If the performance is not staccato vocals, then the slower setting will help the enunciation.

There is some pretty heavy compression going on in both of these examples; it isn't always that heavy.

Modes of Compression

Sidechain Compression

(see Sidechain Compression)
In sidechain, or key compression, a compressor is set to react to an external signal path. When the signal from this path crosses the threshold, the compressor will affect the original audio signal.

Parallel Compression

Parallel compression involves mixing in the compressed signal with some of the dry signal.

I don’t tend to use much parallel compression tactics unless it comes that way from the producer.

- John Hanes, Gearspace

Serial Compression

Serial compression involves using multiple compressors in a row.

Mixer John Hanes explains that he often uses serial compression in tandem with automation:

Compressor 1 (usually 1176 type) gives the vocal a certain pop sound. Compressor 2 does just a bit of smoothing. Much of the actual volume control is from automation; either automate the volume lane, automate the compressor in / out, or copy loud parts to another track with a different compressor setting.

- John Hanes[7]

Philosophy

In my experience, if every compressor reacts exactly the same and sounds identical, then it shrinks the mix.

— Michael Ilbert[8]

Compress for effect, not for volume control.[9]

Get the compressor to make the way you want the vocal to sound, then use volume to adjust levels. Treat the compressor more as a sound design element and less as a volume control method.

- John Hanes[10]

Vocal Production: Can be used while recording to smooth a dynamic recording-- helps a vocalist sing better because they don't have to strain to hear themselves

It can be used to fit elements into mixes by making them sound fuller, or preventing them from being overpowering.
Compression is used to create consistency in performances by smoothing out the level.
It can also be used to tame acoustic imbalances, as certain instruments do not produce uniform levels of sound:

certain notes of a bass guitar resonate more loudly than others, [...] a trumpet plays louder in some registers than in others.[11]

This “compressed” audio makes it easier to hear the nuance of the recorded sound, whether music or the human voice. (from broadcast bridge, quote)

Limiter

A limiter is a compressor with a very high ratio (typically 10:1, or 20:1), used to prevent signals from crossing a certain point (e.g. overloading and clipping).

Functions of Limiters

  • Prevent a signal from crossing a specified level
  • Prevent short-term peaks from reducing a program's average signal level-- especially when audio will be normalized (i.e., Spotify)
  • Prevent high-level, high-frequency peaks from distorting tape or clipping in the digital domain

[6-1]

Philosophy of Limiting

Yes I always mix into the master chain with limiting from the beginning of the mix. If you try to put something on at the end of the process it will undo or change a bunch of the work that you already put in. Mix into the glue!

In this way, you can also control the amount of limiting going on as you work; mix getting too loud? Pull everything down a bit. You can do this during the mix because you'll be able to adjust for how it changes things as you mix. If you do it at the end of the mix, it can change sound of the mix.

Some individual tracks will get limiters as well as necessary, or the volume of parts within the tracks can be ridden down as a sort of manual limiting.

I would say overall there is a lot more compression and volume rides being done than just hitting louder parts with a limiter.

I think that when we were mixing on a console, back in the Teddy Riley days, it was different. We would do the mix and then put the Master Compressor on towards the end. But in those days mixes weren't so loud out of the console and we printed back to 1/2" tape so we needed to get things under control for that.

- John Hanes, Gearspace
