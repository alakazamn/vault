Envelope

In music, the envelope of a waveform is the change in amplitude over the duration of a sound. Envelopes work together with Timbre to create the subjective properties of a sound.[1]

[2]

The envelope of a sound is usually described by four characteristics:[1-1]

  • Attack (A), or the time from the onset of a sound to its peak volume
  • Decay (D), or how fast the sound returns to a sustain level after reaching its peak
  • Sustain (S), or how long a sound will last after the decay
  • Release (R), or how long the sound will continue to play after the note is released.

Some synthesizers, such as Xfer Serum, have an additional hold (H) control, which is the time the peak amplitude is held before the decay kicks in.[2-1]

While envelopes often use peak wave-form values, humans hear the volume of a sound proportionally to the average amplitude of the wave over time. This means that sudden bursts of high amplitude sounds (transients) won't affect the perceived volume of a sound unless the amplitude changes for a long period of time. Instead the character of the sound will be affected.[1-2]

By using a compressor or limiter, an instrument's character can often be modified by changing the dynamics of its envelope without changing its overall timbre.

- David Miles Huber, Modern Recording Techniques[1-3]
